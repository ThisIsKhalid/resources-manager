The 5-step framework · ~45 minutes
STEP 01 ~5 min

Clarify requirements

Ask before drawing. Who uses it? What's the scale — DAU, QPS? Read-heavy or write-heavy? What's the latency SLA? Does it need strong consistency or eventual is fine? What are the out-of-scope parts?

STEP 02 ~5 min

Estimate scale

Back-of-the-envelope math. Calculate QPS, storage growth per year, bandwidth. These numbers drive architecture — 1K QPS needs a different design than 1M QPS. Always round to simple numbers and state assumptions.

STEP 03 ~10 min

High-level design

Draw the obvious: Client → DNS → CDN → Load Balancer → App Servers → DB. Identify the core components first. Don't over-engineer at this stage — sketch, talk, and get agreement before detailing.

STEP 04 ~15 min

Deep dive

Pick 1–2 critical components and go deep. DB schema, caching strategy, data partitioning, API design. The interviewer may guide this — follow their lead. This is where technical depth is demonstrated.

STEP 05 ~10 min

Address bottlenecks

Where's the single point of failure? What breaks first at 10× traffic? What would you monitor? How do you handle partial failures? This shows production awareness — one of the strongest senior signals.

BONUS

Trade-off narration

Every choice needs a "why". Don't say "I'll use Kafka". Say "Kafka because high write throughput and we can tolerate eventual consistency — trade-off is operational complexity and ordering is only per-partition."

⚠ Common interview pitfalls

Going silent and designing alone
Over-engineering from the start
No numbers, no estimations
Ignoring trade-offs
Jumping to solutions before requirements
Forgetting failure scenarios
No consistency model discussion
Treating caching as an afterthought

Senior engineer signals

Proactively discuss trade-offs without being asked
Justify every choice with data or a concrete scenario
Know what you'd do differently at 100× scale
Talk about monitoring, alerting, and how failures are detected
Bring up data consistency models unprompted
Reference real-world systems and how they solved similar problems

Why AI raises the bar for system design
The argument
  • AI doesn't replace system design — it introduces new failure modes: hallucinations, latency spikes, context window limits, cost at scale
  • Writing code gets easier with AI copilots — so the differentiation shifts up the stack to architecture and trade-off thinking
  • AI systems require classic system design AND new ML-specific patterns — double the surface area to master
  • Companies building on LLM APIs still need engineers who can design the infrastructure around them reliably
